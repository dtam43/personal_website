Hoop House Controller

A tool to manage environmental conditions inside Hoop Houses. Created for Western Engineering's Design course. Won the Client Choice Award in the Hoop House category.

A typical Hoop House. The material of the outer hoop traps moisture, allowing crops to be grown even during the winter.

Overview

Our Hoop House controller was created with the goal to help Urban Farms, a local farm that uses Hoop Houses to grow crops year-round. After continued meetings with our client, we determined the following need statement:


Urban Farms needs a user-friendly system to remotely control the temperature and humidity of his two hoop houses within 1Â°C and 5% error over a maximum two-day unattended period during winter months; this will reduce the time he has to stay on-site to manually maintain the houses and allow for better preservation of the crops.


Functional Prototype

To demonstrate our solution, our team created a functional prototype pictured below. The prototype is equipped with sensors that can be installed in the actual Hoop Houses if necessary; a camera as well as a 2-in-1 temperature and humidity sensor. The sensors communicate with a Raspberry Pi processor, which compares the environmental conditions to ideal levels and operates the door under the assumption that doing so will allow cold winter air to flow in.


A purely cosmetic recreation of a Hoop House to house our main components. Comes equipped with a functional door to mimic the process of Hoop House operation.

As the processor is located in-house, the system is capable of functioning fully autonomously. However, should the manager also want remote control and observation over the Hoop Houses, we also included this functionality through a progressive web application that I developed.

Progressive Web Application

The progressive web app serves as a medium between the client and the Hoop House device. It allows the user to view the status of each Hoop House remotely, as well as remotely control operations in the Hoop Houses if necessary.

The main page of the progressive web app. Allows the user to compactly view the conditions of all Hoop Houses.

Clicking on a Hoop House from the main page opens an updated view of the Hoop House as well as controls for the Hoop House.

The editing page for a specific Hoop House. Allows the user to modify the name of the Hoop House as well as the ideal temperature and humidity levels.

Future Improvements

Our project was largely a success; it received the highest score from the Hoop House's manager and won the Client's Choice award. However, there are still improvements that could have been made if we were given more time. One objective that wished to complete was to have the ability to track changes over time. This would have manifested itself in the form of a digital log within our progressive web app that would give the manager an idea of how the Hoop House was being managed if they were to give control to another employee.


Another improvement that we determined after sharing our service with numerous judges and students regards user authentication. Given the nature of our backend server, anyone with the knowledge of the server's IP address can access the Hoop House information and modify it as they see fit. To combat this, all that is needed is to add a wall to the server that prevents anyone from accessing the rest of the content until the passcode is entered.


Overall, I was very satisfied with the completion of this project. Throughout the creation of the project, I learned much about the entire design-thinking process, from empathizing with the client and ideating with my teammates to creating prototypes and refining them.
